\chapter{Evaluation}
\label{ch:evaluation}
% ======================================================================

This chapter reports the evaluation conducted on Evaluation Day (Wednesday 25th
February 2026) and via a follow-up survey. It presents both objective user
acceptance test results and subjective survey findings, and interprets them
against the project's success criteria.

\section{Evaluation Day Setup}
Evaluation Day took place in CS0.03 (Table B01, 13:05--13:45). Four Linux machines plus the author's laptop were used so multiple participants could test in parallel; the application was also deployed publicly for attendees' own devices.

\section{Participants}
Two data sources were collected: a UAT with 10 in-person participants (P1--P10) recorded by an observer, and 13 survey responses via Microsoft Forms (average completion 08:46 minutes, including remote responses). Fewer responses than the preliminary survey (30) were expected because participants needed to try the deployed system within the 40-minute slot.

\section{User Acceptance Testing Tasks}
Three tasks were measured using an observer checklist to provide objective
evidence.

\subsection{Task T1: Auto-schedule Feasibility}
Scenario: at least six stops across one to two days. Evidence recorded:
completion, time-to-finish, non-overlap, and non-zero travel gaps.
9/10 participants completed the task successfully, with a mean completion time of
4:18 (median 4:11). All successful outputs were non-overlapping with non-zero
travel gaps. One participant (P7) timed out due to difficulty locating the
auto-scheduler button without a hint. Full per-participant results are provided
in Appendix~\ref{appx:uat}. This supports Success Criterion SC2.

\subsection{Task T2: Merge Conflict Resolution}
Scenario: pre-seeded repository with two branches producing at least one timing
conflict. Evidence recorded: resolved or not, time-to-resolve, and number of
hints given.
9/10 participants resolved the conflict within 5 minutes, with a mean time of
3:44 and an average of 0.6 hints per participant. One participant (P6) selected
the wrong branch and ran out of time. Full per-participant results are provided
in Appendix~\ref{appx:uat}. This supports Success Criterion SC1.

\subsection{Task T3: Quick Edit and Save Without Git Explanation}
Task T3 measured whether a participant could make a small timeline/map edit and
save without requiring Git vocabulary explanation.
8/10 completed without needing Git explanation. Two participants hesitated around
the mental model of saving and versioning (e.g., asking whether saving creates a
version, or being unsure whether edits were auto-saved). Full per-participant
results are provided in Appendix~\ref{appx:uat}. This supports the value of
Easy mode and indicates that versioning concepts can still raise questions for
some users.

\section{Survey Results}
The Evaluation Day survey collected 13 responses; full per-question results are
reproduced in Appendix~\ref{appx:eval_survey}. Git familiarity among
respondents was mixed: 15\% were not familiar (2/13), 38\% somewhat familiar
(5/13), 23\% comfortable (3/13), and 23\% very comfortable (3/13). Participants
tried a range of features during the session: 11 created or opened a repository,
9 ran the auto-scheduler, 5 attempted branch/compare and merge/conflict
resolution, and 4 used quick route mode.

Likert responses across Core Functionality and Overall Impression were strongly
positive, with responses concentrated in Agree/Strongly Agree. The Git Concept
Understanding section was also positive but showed more neutral responses,
suggesting that the conceptual model is understandable but still benefits from
guidance for some users.

\subsection{System Usability Scale (SUS) Results}
The survey included the ten standard SUS items~\cite{ref_brooke_sus}, yielding a score between 0 and 100. Although only 13 responses were collected, Sauro and Lewis show SUS produces usable estimates with as few as 8--12 respondents~\cite{ref_sauro_lewis_sus}. Individual SUS scores are
provided in Appendix~\ref{appx:uat} (Table~\ref{tab:sus_scores}).

The mean SUS score of \textbf{81.9} (SD~=~10.5) corresponds to an
\textbf{Excellent} adjective rating and a \textbf{Grade~A} on the curved grading
scale proposed by Bangor et~al.~\cite{ref_bangor_sus}, placing GiTrip in
approximately the top~10\% of products benchmarked using SUS and well above the
industry average of~68~\cite{ref_sauro_lewis_sus}.

Per-question contribution scores (0--4 scale) reveal that the strongest items
were Q5 \textit{``Functions were well integrated''} (3.62/4, 90\%) and Q3
\textit{``Easy to use''} (3.54/4, 88\%), indicating that users perceive the
system as coherent and learnable. Q1 \textit{``Would use frequently''} and Q9
\textit{``Felt confident''} both scored 3.46/4 (87\%), suggesting strong
engagement and self-efficacy.

The weakest items were Q6 \textit{``Too much inconsistency''} and Q10
\textit{``Needed to learn a lot''}, both at 2.92/4 (73\%), alongside Q4
\textit{``Would need technical support''} at 3.00/4 (75\%). These suggest that
while overall usability is excellent, there is some perceived inconsistency in
the interface and a moderate onboarding learning curve --- consistent with the
qualitative feedback about Git concepts requiring learning overhead.

Eleven of thirteen respondents scored at or above 72.5; two (R1 and R2) scored below~68, possibly reflecting lower Git familiarity, though the sample prevents subgroup analysis.

\subsection{Qualitative Feedback}
Free-text responses provide additional evidence of perceived value and pain
points.

Respondents most frequently praised the auto-scheduler's usefulness and edge-case handling (e.g., clearly showing unscheduled stops with reasons). Several highlighted the novelty of Git-style merge conflict resolution in a non-code context and the educational algorithm visualisation.

Some users found Git concepts required learning overhead, and merge conflicts were confusing without guidance---consistent with UAT hint patterns. One respondent identified a routing limitation for special transport modes (e.g., ferries).

Most respondents indicated they would adopt GiTrip, citing collaboration and ``keeping everything in one place'' as key benefits. Blockers were perceived complexity for casual trips and routing constraints for niche transport.

\section{Discussion Against Success Criteria}
Overall, the evaluation provides strong evidence that core objectives were
achieved. The auto-scheduler reliably produced feasible plans for typical
scenarios, supporting SC2. Merge conflict resolution was achievable by most
participants within the target time, supporting SC1. The SUS score of 81.9
(Grade~A, Excellent) validates the Easy mode approach and overall usability,
while per-question analysis highlights interface consistency and onboarding as
remaining improvement areas (SC3 partially met, with known limitations).

\section{Threats to Validity}
The evaluation has limitations typical of student project evaluations:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Sample bias:} many participants were likely students with
        above-average technical literacy, which may inflate usability scores
        relative to the general population.
  \item \textbf{Small sample size:} only 13 survey responses were collected due
        to the time restriction of the 40-minute Evaluation Day slot. While
        Sauro and Lewis~\cite{ref_sauro_lewis_sus} show SUS is reliable at this
        size, the sample prevents confident subgroup analysis (e.g.\ comparing
        scores by Git familiarity level).
  \item \textbf{Demonstration context:} users may perform differently in real
        trip planning over longer time horizons.
\end{itemize}
